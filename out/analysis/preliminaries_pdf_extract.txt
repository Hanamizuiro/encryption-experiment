  Hill  Cipher  
The  Hill  cipher  is  a  classical  polygraphic  substitution  cipher  developed  by  Lester  S.  Hill  in  1929.  
The
 
Hill

The

In

In  mathematical  form,  encryption  is  defined  as:  
$$\mathbf{C}  =  \mathbf{K}  \cdot  \mathbf{P}  \pmod{m}$$  
Where  
●  \mathbf{P}  is  the  plaintext  vector,  ●  \mathbf{K}   is  the  invertible  key  matrix,  ●  \mathbf{C}  is  the  ciphertext  vector,  and  ●  m  is  the  modulus,  which  is  commonly  26  for  a  text  and  256  for  8-bit  grayscale  images.  
A  Hill  cipher  is  only  valid  if  the  key  matrix  $\mathbf{K}$  is  invertible  in  $\mathbb{Z}_m$.  This  
requires
 
that
 
the
 
determinant
 
of
 
the
 
matrix
 
and
 
the
 
modulus
 
are
 
coprime:
 
$$\gcd(\det(\mathbf{K}),  m)  =  1$$  
Decryption  is  performed  using  the  inverse  key  matrix:  
$$\mathbf{P}  =  \mathbf{K}^{-1}  \cdot  \mathbf{C}  \pmod{m}$$  
When  this  is  applied  to  a  digital  image,  pixel  value  information  is  arranged  in  vectors  or  matrix  
blocks
 
and
 
encrypted
 
using
 
the
 
same
 
linear
 
transformation
 
principle.
 
Due
 
to
 
the
 
high
 
redundancy
 
of
 
image
 
information,
 
which
 
is
 
caused
 
by
 
the
 
high
 
correlation
 
of
 
pixel
 
information,
 
the
 
Hill
 
Cipher
 
adds
 
a
 
diffusion
 
effect
 
by
 
mixing
 
pixel
 
value
 
information
 
in
 
the
 
matrix
 
blocks,
 
thus
 
breaking
 
the
 
correlation
 
of
 
the
 
pixel
 
information.
 
 
The  decryption  process  is  highly  sensitive  to  numerical  inaccuracies.  Inaccurate  information  
introduced
 
to
 
the
 
ciphertext
 
during
 
transmission
 
can

the
 
matrix

of
 
the

This
 
is

to

of
 
the
 
solver
 
when

effect
 
of
 
stochastic
 
corruption.

LU  Decomposition  
The  LU  factorization  is  a  particularly  useful  factorization  of  a  matrix  A  where  A=LU.  
●  L  is  a  lower  triangular  matrix.  ●  U  is  an  upper  triangular  matrix.  
Theorem  1:  LU  Factorization  Without  Pivoting  
Suppose  that  Gaussian  elimination  can  be  applied  to  the  system  Ax=b  without  any  row

●  U  is  the  resulting  upper-triangular  matrix  from  the  elimination  process.  ●  L  is  the  unit  lower-triangular  matrix  whose  subdiagonal  entries  are  the  multipliers  
$m_{j,i}=\frac{a_{ji}^{(i)}}{a_{ii}^{(i)}}$.
 
LU  Factorization  with  Row  Interchanges   
For  any  nonsingular  matrix  A,  there  exists  a  permutation  matrix  P  such  that  
PA  =  LU,  
where  L  is  unit  lower  triangular  matrix  and  U  is  upper  triangular  matrix.

In  general,  P^T  L  is  not  lower  triangular  unless  P=I.
