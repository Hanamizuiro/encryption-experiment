\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a numerical-analysis benchmark for block-based image decryption using the 24-image Kodak lossless benchmark set \cite{kodak-lossless}. Each image is resized to $128 \times 128$, partitioned into $8 \times 8$ blocks, encrypted with dense and sparse key matrices, corrupted with additive Gaussian noise, and then decrypted by Gaussian Elimination with partial pivoting, LU Decomposition, Gauss-Seidel with relaxation, and GMRES. The comparison focuses on numerical robustness, reconstruction quality, convergence behavior, and runtime under controlled perturbations. The results show that LU decomposition is the fastest method overall, with median solve times of about $244.71$ ms for dense keys and $246.03$ ms for sparse keys, while GMRES delivers the strongest iterative convergence, achieving a full-convergence rate of $1.0$ for both matrix families across all tested noise levels. All four solvers maintain a solve-success rate of $1.0$, but Gauss-Seidel falls to an overall full-convergence rate of $0.833$ because some zero-noise runs reach the iteration cap before satisfying the strict stopping criterion. These findings indicate that LU offers the best runtime efficiency for repeated block solves, whereas GMRES provides the most reliable convergence behavior under stochastic corruption.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Image data is common in many computing applications, such as medical diagnostics, remote sensing, and multimedia communication. In many of these image-based computing tasks, the manipulation and analysis of images can be achieved by solving a system of linear equations, as defined by the model, the image, or the process. In this context, the linear equation can be expressed as a matrix, which can be either dense or sparse, depending on the image data and the model. Gaussian elimination, LU decomposition, Gauss-Seidel method with relaxation, and hybrid Krylov subspace methods such as GMRES are some of the fundamental numerical analysis techniques that can be applied to compute the solution to the linear equation.

However, in real-world problems, image data is often subject to random perturbations such as noise, transmission error, compression artifacts, and storage corruption. Small perturbations in the data may cause significant effects on the computation results. This sensitivity is conceptually similar to the avalanche effect in cryptographic systems, where a small change in the input causes a significant change in the output. In image encryption studies, the robustness of the diffusion properties is exploited to enhance the avalanche effect, yet this phenomenon also causes severe error propagation on the corrupted encrypted images, which makes the recovery of the image difficult or impossible \cite{noura-2017}. This shows how small numerical perturbations may cause severe distortion in the image data.

Moreover, in the field of research on image encryption, the significance of robustness in the presence of differential modifications is stressed, while at the same time, the danger of over-sensitivity to such modifications is also pointed out \cite{alghamdi-munir-2024}. Even though the major concern in research on image encryption is centered on security issues, the research shows that computational studies face a fundamental problem which requires assessment of image processing tasks to be evaluated for both performance and their ability to withstand noisy or damaged conditions. Similarly, in numerical linear algebra, any modification in the elements of a matrix could affect the overall behavior of the elimination process.

These considerations highlight the importance of systematically comparing how different numerical solvers respond to stochastic corruption in image-derived linear systems. In particular, the current study aims to test the baseline stability of the numerical solver that is based on the algorithm of Gaussian Elimination with Partial Pivoting, the block-reusability efficiency of the numerical solver that is based on the algorithm of LU Decomposition, the smoothing effects of the numerical solver that is based on the algorithm of Gauss-Seidel with Relaxation, and the robustness of the numerical solver that is based on the algorithm of Hybrid GMRES.

\subsection{Hill Cipher}
The Hill cipher is a classical polygraphic substitution cipher developed by Lester S. Hill in 1929. The Hill cipher was based on the principles of linear algebra. The Hill cipher was different from other substitution ciphers like the Caesar cipher, which was based on single-character substitution. In the Hill cipher, blocks of characters were considered to be vectors and were multiplied by a matrix to perform substitution.

In mathematical form, encryption is defined as
\[
\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{K} \cdot \mathbf{P} \pmod{m}
\]
Where
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathbf{P}$ is the plaintext vector,
\item $\mathbf{K}$ is the invertible key matrix,
\item $\mathbf{C}$ is the ciphertext vector, and
\item $m$ is the modulus, which is commonly $26$ for a text and $256$ for 8-bit grayscale images.
\end{itemize}

A Hill cipher is only valid if the key matrix $\mathbf{K}$ is invertible in $\mathbb{Z}_m$. This requires that the determinant of the matrix and the modulus are coprime:
\[
\gcd(\det(\mathbf{K}), m) = 1.
\]
Decryption is performed using the inverse key matrix:
\[
\mathbf{P} = \mathbf{K}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{C} \pmod{m}.
\]
When this is applied to a digital image, pixel value information is arranged in vectors or matrix blocks and encrypted using the same linear transformation principle. Due to the high redundancy of image information, which is caused by the high correlation of pixel information, the Hill Cipher adds a diffusion effect by mixing pixel value information in the matrix blocks, thus breaking the correlation of the pixel information. The decryption process is highly sensitive to numerical inaccuracies. Inaccurate information introduced to the ciphertext during transmission can affect the matrix inversion, leading to degradation of the image. This is relevant to the robustness of the solver when analyzing the effect of stochastic corruption.

\subsection{Dense and Sparse Matrices}
In this research, the linear equation can be expressed as a matrix, which can be either dense or sparse, depending on the image data and the model. A dense matrix contains mostly nonzero entries, whereas a sparse matrix contains many zero entries. This distinction matters because matrix structure affects storage, arithmetic cost, coupling between variables, and the convergence behavior of iterative solvers under stochastic corruption.

\subsection{Numerical Solvers}

\subsubsection{Gaussian Elimination}
Gaussian elimination with backward substitution is an algorithm to solve a system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ variables represented as $Ax=b$, where $A$ is the coefficient matrix, $x$ is the vector of variables, and $b$ is the constant vector. The main goal of the Gaussian elimination algorithm is to transform the augmented matrix of the system into an equivalent upper triangular matrix form. This is achieved by a sequence of elimination operations on the variables of the system through three basic row operations that do not change the solution set of the system of equations: scaling, row replacement, and row interchanging.

\begin{itemize}
\item Scaling: $(\lambda \, E_i) \rightarrow (E_i)$ where $\lambda \neq 0$.
\item Row replacement: $(E_i + \lambda \, E_j) \rightarrow (E_i)$.
\item Row interchanging: $(E_i) \leftrightarrow (E_j)$.
\end{itemize}

The Gaussian elimination algorithm has two main steps:

1) Elimination Phase: The main goal of the elimination phase is to eliminate the elements below the main diagonal of the augmented matrix of the system through the application of the basic row operations. If the element in the $i$th row and $i$th column, that is, $a_{ii}$, is zero, the rows $E_i$ and $E_j$ should be interchanged, where $E_i$ is the current row of the augmented matrix, and $E_j$ is the row in the augmented matrix such that the element in the $j$th column of $E_j$ is not zero.

2) Backward Substitution Phase: The Gaussian elimination algorithm transforms the system of linear equations into an upper triangular form, after which the variables $x_n, x_{n-1}, \ldots, x_1$ can be found sequentially starting from the last equation up to the first equation.

The computational cost of the Gaussian elimination algorithm for the system of $n$ linear equations in $n$ variables can be found to be of the order of $O(n^3)$ for the multiplications/divisions as well as the additions/subtractions.

\paragraph{Stability and Noise Sensitivity}
A key component of the experimental protocol is the Noise Injection stress test, where Gaussian White Noise is added to the encrypted blocks $(C' = C + \varepsilon)$.

\paragraph{Rounding Errors}
As the computational cost of the Gaussian Elimination method is given by $O(n^3)$, the rounding errors that occur during the elimination of $64$ variables resulting from the $8 \times 8$ matrix can cause salt-and-pepper noise to be introduced in the reconstructed image if the key matrix is poorly conditioned.

\paragraph{Partial Pivoting}
To enhance the robustness of the system, the algorithm makes use of the Partial Pivoting method. By choosing the pivot with the maximum value during the scaling operation, the effects of the injected random noise are reduced to ensure the accuracy of the baseline recovery for comparison with the Gauss-Seidel method.

\subsubsection{LU Decomposition}
The LU factorization is a particularly useful factorization of a matrix $A$ where $A=LU$.
\begin{itemize}
\item $L$ is a lower triangular matrix.
\item $U$ is an upper triangular matrix.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Theorem 1: LU Factorization Without Pivoting}
Suppose that Gaussian elimination can be applied to the system $Ax=b$ without any row interchange operations. Then, matrix $A$ can be written as the product of a lower triangular matrix $L$ and an upper triangular matrix $U$, such that $A=LU$.
\begin{itemize}
\item $U$ is the resulting upper-triangular matrix from the elimination process.
\item $L$ is the unit lower-triangular matrix whose subdiagonal entries are the multipliers
\end{itemize}
\[
m_{j,i} = \frac{a_{ji}^{(i)}}{a_{ii}^{(i)}}.
\]

\paragraph{LU Factorization with Row Interchanges}
For any nonsingular matrix $A$, there exists a permutation matrix $P$ such that
\[
PA = LU,
\]
where $L$ is a unit lower triangular matrix and $U$ is an upper triangular matrix. Since permutation matrices satisfy $P^{-1} = P^T$, one may write
\[
A = (P^{-1}L)U = (P^TL)U.
\]
In general, $P^TL$ is not lower triangular unless $P=I$.

\paragraph{Application in Image Decryption}
In the context of digital image security, LU Decomposition is commonly used because it is more efficient in terms of computation when solving a system of linear equations. In block-type encryption techniques, it is assumed that a digital image is divided into small blocks of pixels, such as $8 \times 8$ in size. If a key matrix $A$ is employed in image encryption, it is only factorized into $L$ and $U$ matrices once. This way many blocks are processed efficiently without having to perform a time-consuming $O(n^3)$ algorithm of Gaussian elimination for every block. Incorporating a permutation matrix $P$ ensures that row interchanges are allowed during the encryption process, thus preventing zero pivot elements and thereby enhancing numerical stability in the decryption mechanism.

\subsubsection{Gauss-Seidel with Relaxation}
The Gauss-Seidel with relaxation method, also known as the Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR) method, is an iterative method used to solve systems of linear equations. This method is derived from the fundamental theories of numerical analysis and matrix computation. This method is different from the standard Gauss-Seidel method, which uses only the latest values obtained during the solution computation. The solution obtained by the Gauss-Seidel method with relaxation is adjusted by a relaxation parameter to accelerate convergence.

In mathematical form,
\[
(D - \omega L)x^{(k+1)} = \omega b + (1 - \omega)Dx^{(k)},
\]
where:
\begin{itemize}
\item $D$ is the diagonal matrix containing the diagonal elements of the coefficient matrix,
\item $L$ is the strictly lower triangular matrix containing the elements below the diagonal,
\item $x^{(k)}$ is the solution vector from the previous iteration,
\item $x^{(k+1)}$ is the updated solution vector after the current iteration,
\item $b$ is the constant vector of the system of equations, and
\item $\omega$ is the relaxation parameter that controls the speed of convergence.
\end{itemize}
The coefficient matrix of the given system of linear equations is represented as a sum of three matrices: $D$, a diagonal matrix; $L$, a strictly lower triangular matrix; and $U$, a strictly upper triangular matrix. The relaxation parameter $\omega$ is used to adjust the solution obtained by the Gauss-Seidel method. If $1 < \omega < 2$, over-relaxation is achieved by this method, which can converge faster to the solution by moving more aggressively towards it.

This method is mostly used for solving large linear systems that come up as a result of mathematical models such as differential equations and grids. This method is appropriate because it is done iteratively and can be carried out on a digital computer. However, it has been noted that the method's performance largely depends on the relaxation parameter used. If an appropriate relaxation parameter is not used, it can diverge or converge slowly. Therefore, it is important to ensure that an appropriate relaxation parameter is used to ensure convergence when solving large systems numerically. 

In image decryption, it increases the speed of the convergence of the iterative solvers in reversing encryption processes modeled for large systems of linear equations. By introducing an algorithm that uses relaxation parameters, this enhances the stability and speed of reconstructing the original pixel values from encrypted data, which is particularly effective in chaotic or transformation-based encryption schemes \cite{burden-faires-2015,frankel-1950,varga-1962,young-1950,trefethen-bau-1997}.

\subsubsection{GMRES (Hybrid)}
The Generalized Minimal Residual method (GMRES) is an iterative Krylov subspace algorithm introduced by Saad and Schultz in 1986 to address large nonsymmetric systems of linear equations $Ax=b$. It is considered a non-stationary iterative solver with a unique trade-off in terms of efficiency and precision.

\paragraph{Krylov Subspace and the Arnoldi Process}
Unlike stationary methods like Gauss-Seidel, GMRES constructs an approximate solution $x_n$ from the $n$th Krylov subspace, defined as
\[
K_n(A,r_0) = \operatorname{span}\{r_0, Ar_0, A^2r_0, \ldots, A^{n-1}r_0\},
\]
where $r_0 = b - Ax_0$ is the initial residual. The algorithm uses the Arnoldi process, an orthogonalization method that follows the modified Gram-Schmidt procedure to build an orthonormal basis $\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}$ for the subspace. This gives an upper Hessenberg matrix $H_n$ that represents the projection of matrix $A$ onto $K_n$.

\paragraph{Residual Minimization}
The defining characteristic of GMRES is its optimality property, which is that at each iteration $n$, it finds the $x_n \in x_0 + K_n$ that minimizes the Euclidean norm of the residual,
\[
\|r_n\|_2 = \min_{p \in \mathcal{P}_n,\, p(0)=1} \|p(A)r_0\|_2.
\]
The minimization process is achieved by solving a compact least-squares problem of size $(n+1) \times n$ involving the Hessenberg matrix. Within the context of the matrix-based image transform, the decryption of the pixel matrix into the most mathematically precise representation possible within the chosen subspace dimensionality is guaranteed.

\paragraph{Computational Constraints and Restarting}
Another important factor to keep in mind with GMRES is the fact that the computational cost and memory requirements are linearly proportional to the iteration count, denoted as $n$. To reduce the memory overhead involved with the computation of the high-resolution image matrices, the restarted GMRES, denoted as $\mathrm{GMRES}(k)$, is often utilized. Here, the current approximation is utilized as the new initial guess after $k$ iterations, and the cycle starts again. This reduces the orthogonalization bottleneck without compromising the ability to solve non-normal matrices, which are often the result of complex encryption keys \cite{nachtigal-gmres-1992}.

\subsection{Image Quality Metrics}
The image quality metrics used in this research are Mean Square Error (MSE) and Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR). These serve as benchmarks for assessing solver performance under stochastic data corruption.

For an original image $I$ and reconstructed image $\hat{I}$ with $N$ pixels,
\[
\operatorname{MSE}(I, \hat{I}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{N}(I_k - \hat{I}_k)^2.
\]
Using unit-range image normalization, PSNR is reported as
\[
\operatorname{PSNR}(I, \hat{I}) = 10\log_{10}\left(\frac{1}{\operatorname{MSE}(I, \hat{I})}\right).
\]
Lower MSE and higher PSNR indicate better reconstruction quality.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Solvers}
The notebook implements the following methods:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Gaussian Elimination with partial pivoting}: direct method used as the classical baseline for row-wise elimination stability \cite{chapra-canale-2010}.
\item \textbf{LU Decomposition (Doolittle)}: factorization-based method that amortizes the matrix factorization across repeated block solves \cite{chapra-canale-2010,lu-notes}.
\item \textbf{Gauss-Seidel with relaxation}: iterative method used to study convergence and noise sensitivity in repeated fixed-point updates.
\item \textbf{GMRES}: Krylov-subspace hybrid method added to match the target plan and to provide a modern iterative reference.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{From Pseudocode to Code}
The notebook was rewritten so that each solver mirrors the structure of the supplied pseudocode rather than hiding the numerical work inside a library call.

For Gaussian Elimination, the pseudocode specifies partial pivoting with row exchanges, multiplier storage, and back substitution. The notebook implementation follows that pattern directly: it builds an augmented matrix, tracks row order through a pointer array, swaps pivot rows, stores the elimination multipliers below the diagonal, and then performs explicit backward substitution to recover the block solution.

For LU factorization, the pseudocode uses the Doolittle-style in-place decomposition. The notebook follows the same sequence: each column is normalized by the pivot, the Schur-complement update is applied to the trailing submatrix, and the resulting packed matrix is reused by a separate forward/back substitution routine for every encrypted image block.

For Gauss-Seidel, the pseudocode repeatedly updates the current iterate using the newest available values, checks an approximate stopping condition, and records whether the iterate reached the iteration cap. The notebook reflects that structure exactly. It normalizes by the diagonal, applies the relaxation factor during each component update, measures a relative residual after every sweep, and optionally stores the residual history for the convergence-trace plots.

For GMRES, the notebook intentionally delegates the Krylov basis construction to SciPy because the project goal is comparison rather than re-deriving Arnoldi from scratch. Even so, the surrounding experiment logic mirrors the pseudocode plan: the same noisy block systems are passed to the solver, iteration counts are recorded through a callback, and the resulting residuals are exported in the same artifact format as the hand-coded methods.

\subsection{How the Notebook Works}
The code path from raw Kodak image to final table is fully deterministic once the random seed is fixed.
\begin{enumerate}
\item The configuration cell selects the Kodak subset, the block size, the noise levels, and the solver tolerances.
\item The dataset loader converts each image to grayscale, resizes it to $128 \times 128$, stores provenance metadata, and writes a contact sheet for the exact 24 images used in the run.
\item Two key matrices are generated: one dense and one sparse, each with a diagonal boost so that the block systems remain numerically solvable across all repeated experiments.
\item Each image is split into $8 \times 8$ blocks, flattened into $64$-vectors, encrypted by matrix multiplication, then perturbed with Gaussian noise.
\item Every solver is run on the same noisy blocks. The code measures reconstruction fidelity, residuals, runtime, mean iterations, and whether any block solve reached the iteration cap.
\item The export stage aggregates those per-image records into CSV summaries, LaTeX tables, metric figures, convergence plots, and wall-of-decryption panels that the paper includes directly.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Results Highlights}
Two high-level outcomes stand out in the regenerated summaries.

First, among the four study solvers, LU is the speed leader. Averaged over all noise levels, its median solve time is about $244.71$ ms for dense keys and $246.03$ ms for sparse keys, substantially ahead of Gaussian elimination, GMRES, and Gauss-Seidel.

Second, the strongest convergence behavior among the iterative methods is delivered by GMRES. Its full-convergence rate is $1.0$ for both dense and sparse matrices across all tested noise levels, whereas Gauss-Seidel drops to an overall full-convergence rate of $0.833$ because some zero-noise cases hit the iteration ceiling before satisfying the strict stopping rule. At the same time, all methods achieve a solve-success rate of $1.0$, so the practical difference is not reconstruction failure but how reliably each method satisfies the configured convergence criterion.

\section{Discussion}
Three practical observations motivate the current design.

First, LU decomposition is expected to remain highly competitive whenever the same key matrix is reused across many blocks, because the factorization cost is paid once and the subsequent solves are comparatively cheap.

Second, Gauss-Seidel and GMRES provide complementary iterative viewpoints. Gauss-Seidel exposes convergence behavior directly through residual traces, while GMRES offers a hybrid Krylov-based reference for the same noisy block systems. The updated reporting separates \emph{solve success} from \emph{full convergence}. That distinction matters most at zero noise, where Gauss-Seidel still reconstructs every block correctly but some runs terminate at the configured iteration ceiling before satisfying the strict stopping test.

Third, the dense-versus-sparse comparison matters numerically even outside classical conditioning summaries. Two matrices can have similar condition numbers and still differ in residual propagation, convergence behavior, and runtime profile once the solver and noise process are fixed.
